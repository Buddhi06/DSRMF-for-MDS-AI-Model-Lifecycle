Phase 1: Step 2:
Risk Evaluation

Risk evaluation is the process of assessing each identified risk to determine whether security risk reduction is required, according to the defined "risk acceptability criteria".

In risk treatment, the risk acceptability criteria are used to determine whether the proposed risk treatment is sufficient to achieve an acceptable level of risk or if further risk treatment is necessary.

When defining the risk acceptability criteria, the following influencing factors need to be considered (this list is not exhaustive):

  • Organisational objectives
  • Organisational opportunities
  • Legal and regulatory aspects
  • Operational activities
  • Technological constraints
  • Financial constraints
  • Processes
  • Supplier relationships
The criteria for risk acceptance can be defined as a value above which risks are deemed tolerable (threshold value). If the value “moderate” is chosen as the threshold:
  • All risks with a value of very low, or low are considered - "acceptable"
  • All the risks with a value of moderate are considered - "tolerable"
  • All risks with a value of high or very high are considered - "unacceptable".

As shown in the table below, three colours — red, amber, and green — can be used to represent three risk evaluation grades.

Example of evaluation scale combined with three-color risk matrix

Level of Risk Risk Evaluation Description
Very low and Low (Green) Acceptable as is The risk can be accepted without further action.
Moderate (Amber) Tolerable under control A follow-up in terms of risk management should be conducted and actions should be set up for continuous improvement over the medium and long term. Treatment options include:
  • Conducting regular reviews to check whether the risk levels have changed.
  • Maintaining a risk register which lists all tolerable risks
  • Define Key Risk Indicators (KRI) and analyse the trends in KRI data
High and very high (Red) Unacceptable Measures for reducing the risk should absolutely be taken in the short-term. Otherwise, all or a portion of the activity should be refused.

Finally, all acceptable, tolerable, and unacceptable risks should be documented in the security risk assessment report along with the rationale.

Prepare the Risk Assessment Report

Upon completion of the risk assessment, the results should be documented in the risk assessment report.
The report should contain the following sections:

  1. An executive summary
  2. The main body containing detailed risk assessment results
  3. Supporting appendices

Executive Summary

  • The date of the risk assessment
  • Summarise the purpose of the risk assessment
  • Describe the scope of the risk assessment
  • State whether the risk assessment is initial or subsequent. If a subsequent risk assessment, state the circumstances that prompted the update and include a reference to the previous Risk Assessment Report.
  • Describe the overall level of risk (e.g., Very Low, Low, Moderate, High or Very High).
  • List the number of risks identified for each level of risk (e.g., Very Low, Low, Moderate, High or Very High).

Body of the Report

  • Describe the purpose of the risk assessment
  • Identify assumptions and constraints
  • Describe the selected risk assessment and analysis approaches and rationale for the selection
  • List of assets identified
  • List of threats and vulnerabilities along with their descriptions
  • The adverse impact of each threat and vulnerability
  • The risk acceptability criteria used
  • Summary of the risk assessment result including the threats that fall into each level (i.e., very high, high, moderate, low and very low) and list of threats that are acceptable, tolerable and unacceptable with rationale.

Appendices

  • List of references and sources of information
  • List the team or individuals conducting the risk assessment, including their contact information
