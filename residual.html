Phase 2: Step 3:
Residual Risk Evaluation

Residual risk is the risk that remains after risk control measures have been applied.

For example, encryption is a risk controls that can be used to mitigate data breaches in MDS AI models. However, residual risk can remain even after implementing encryption, due to sophisticated hacking techniques or human errors.

Therefore, residual risk should be re-evaluated for each threat once the risk controls have been applied. Additional risk controls may need to be implemented if the residual risk is deemed unacceptable.

Residual risk can be determined by following the steps below

Step 1: Determine the inherent risk

  • Inherent risk is the level of risk that exists before implementing any risk control measures (also known as risk before controls or the gross risk).
  • Risk scores calculated during Step 1.3 (Risk Estimation) of Phase 1 (Risk Analysis) is considered as the inherent risk score (They are the level of risks that exist before applying any of the risk controls).

Step 2: Determine the Control effectiveness

  • Control effectiveness referes to the degree to which implemented risk controls reduce the likelihood or impact of the threat, expressed as a percentage or on a scale (0-1).
  • To identify the control effectiveness the following approaches can be used:
    1. Expert judgement - Based on the opinion of AI model security experts
    2. Penetration testing - Penetration testing
    3. Historical Data - Use past incidents data
  • Based on these approaches, a percentage value or a score between (0-1) should be assigned to represent the control effectiveness.

Step 3: Determine the residual risk

After indentifying the inherent risk and control effectiveness, the residual risk can be determined using the formula below:

Residual Risk = Inherant Risk * (1-Control Effectiveness)
